The Sama-Badjao Arts

Explore the artistic traditions of the Sama–Badjao, from intricate wood carvings and boat designs to geometric Okir patterns in textiles and metalwork, reflecting their daily life and cultural heritage.

Boat Carving

The Sama–Badjao carve their lepa boats and grave markers with intricate plant and animal motifs, reflecting their deep connection to the sea and nature. These carvings are not only decorative but also carry symbolic meanings, telling stories of their heritage, beliefs, and community identity. Skilled artisans carefully shape each piece by hand, preserving traditional techniques passed down through generations.

Okir Designs

Okir refers to the intricate geometric and flowing patterns used by the Sama–Badjao in wood, textiles, and metalwork. These designs often feature stylized plant, animal, and wave motifs, symbolizing nature, spirituality, and cultural identity. Found on boats, household items, clothing, and ceremonial objects, Okir patterns showcase the artistry, creativity, and craftsmanship of the Sama–Badjao people.

Tepoor Tepoh

It refers to both the craft of mat weaving and the finished mat itself, traditionally made from pandan, buri, and other native fibers. The mat features two layers: the lapis (underside), which is simply woven, and the diam (topside), which is crafted from strips dyed in vibrant shades of green, orange, red, violet, and blue.

Patadyong

A traditional wraparound skirt that is large enough to fit any person and is worn by both men and women as a skirt or a gown tucked in place at the chest level. It can serve as putung (headcover), waistband, sash, blanket, hammock, shoulder bag, cradle, pouch, hood, or pillow.

Saruk

A man’s hat that is constructed from woven nipa or pandan (pandanus) leaves, which keep its natural milky hue. Geometric, spiral, or concentric lines are occasionally painted on the cap.

Sunduk

A grave marker that is carved in the shape of an animal, often a dragon, serpent, seahorse, or bird and believed to guide the spirit into the afterlife. It stands about 60 centimeters tall and is mounted on a sturdy wooden base that is placed directly over the grave.

The Sama-Badjao Music and Dance

Explore the vibrant musical and dance traditions of the Sama–Badjao, from rhythmic percussive ensembles and haunting vocal chants to graceful interpretive movements that echo the rhythms of the sea, all reflecting their daily life, spiritual beliefs, and rich cultural heritage.

Kulintang

A row of small, horizontally placed, tuned pot gongs played melodically, often by women.

Agung

Large, vertically suspended gongs that serve as the rhythmic foundation of the ensemble and are typically played by men.

Gabbang

is a bamboo/ wooden xylophone, one of the musical instruments of the Sama Bajau, normally of seventeenth keys of graduated lengths mounted on a trapezoidal box. It is played by direct striking using a wooden mallet either as a solo instrument or in an accompaniment to singing and dancing.

Igal

A significant dance, particularly during marriages, that often features dancers with metal fingernails.

The Sama-Badjao Traditional Songs

Explore the traditional songs of the Sama–Badjao, from melodic chants and sea-inspired narratives to ritual music passed down through generations, capturing their lived experiences, values, and deep connection to the ocean.

Kanduli

A traditional Badjao celebration song used in rituals and gatherings.

Tenggayun

A traditional Sama-Badjao song that reflects their connection to the sea.

Dilaut

A song about life on the sea, capturing the essence of Badjao maritime culture.

Nasheed - Kun Anta

A spiritual song reflecting Islamic influences in Badjao culture.

Runsai

A traditional melody often played during cultural celebrations.

The Sama-Badjao Traditions

Explore the enduring traditions of the Sama–Badjao, from community rituals and seafaring customs to symbolic practices that honor their ancestry, reflecting their way of life and cultural identity.

Ancestor Worship

They practice forms of ancestor worship, making offerings to spirits and deceased relatives.

Offerings

They make offerings to the "God of the Sea," known as Omboh Dilaut.

Traditional Attire

The traditional patadjong, a wraparound skirt, is a key part of their traditional attire.
